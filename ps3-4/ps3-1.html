1. Show that all common citizens who receive a signal \(s_{i,C}=1\) vote for \(P\) for all values of \(\alpha\) and common citizens who receive a signal \(s_{i,C} = 0\) vote for \(P\) if and only if \(\alpha<1/2\).

    As we have shown in [Q4],

    For commoners receiving \(s_{i,C}=1\), after signal \(s_{i,C}\), a common voter \(i\) casts a ballot for \(P\) if and only if:

    • \(\mu(s_{i,C}, x_A, x_B, x_P)<\frac{1-|\delta|}{2}\) if \(x_A=x_B=1\) and \(x_P=0\).

    • \(\mu(s_{i,C}, x_A, x_B, x_P)>\frac{1+|\delta|}{2}\) if \(x_A=x_B=0\) and \(x_P=1\).

    The second case aligns more with settings in [Q5]. In this case, it requires \(\mu(s_{i,C},0,0,1)>\frac{1+|\delta|}{2}\).

    • When \(s_{i,C}=1\), \(\mu(1,0,0,1)=\frac{(1-\alpha)p^2}{(1-\alpha)p^2+\alpha(1-p)^2}\).

      We need to show \(\frac{(1-\alpha)p^2}{(1-\alpha)p^2+\alpha(1-p)^2}-\frac{1+|\delta|}{2}>0\).

      This reduces to \((1-|\delta|)(1-\alpha)p^2-(1+|\delta|)\alpha(1-p)^2>0\).

      • When \(\alpha>0.5\), \(|\delta|<\frac{2\alpha-1}{2}\).

        \[\begin{align*} &(1-|\delta|)(1-\alpha)p^2-(1+|\delta|)\alpha(1-p)^2 \\ >&(\frac{1}{2}-\alpha)(1-\alpha)p^2-(\frac{1}{2}+\alpha)\alpha(1-p)^2 \\ >&(\frac{1}{2}-p)(1-p)^2-(\frac{1}{2}+p)p(1-p)^2 \\ =&p(1-p)[(\frac{1}{2}-p)p-(\frac{1}{2}+p)(1-p)] \\ =&-\frac{1}{2}p(1-p) \end{align*}\]

        This expression is positive when \(p\in(0,1)\). As in the setting, \(p>0.5\), therefore the condition is met in this case.

      • When \(\alpha<0.5\), \(|\delta|<\frac{1-2\alpha}{2}\).

        \[\begin{align*} &(1-|\delta|)(1-\alpha)p^2-(1+|\delta|)\alpha(1-p)^2\\ =&(\frac{1}{2}+\alpha)(1-\alpha)p^2-(\frac{3}{2}-\alpha)\alpha(1-p)^2\\ =&(\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{2}\alpha-\alpha^2)-(\frac{3}{2}\alpha-\alpha^2)(1-p)^2\\ \end{align*}\]

        Since \(p>1/2\), \(p^2>(1-p)^2\). we only need to show \(\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{2}\alpha-\alpha^2>\frac{3}{2}\alpha-\alpha^2\).

        \[\begin{align*} \frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{2}\alpha-\alpha^2&>\frac{3}{2}\alpha-\alpha^2 \\ \frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{2}\alpha&>\frac{3}{2}\alpha \\ \alpha&<0.5 \end{align*}\]

        This is therefore also true.

      Therefore, when \(s_{i,C}=1\), \(\mu(1,0,0,1)=\frac{(1-\alpha)p^2}{(1-\alpha)p^2+\alpha(1-p)^2}>\frac{1+|\delta|}{2}\).

    • When \(s_{i,C}=0\), \(\mu(0,0,0,1)=1-\alpha\).

      To show \(\mu(0,0,0,1)>\frac{1+|\delta|}{2}\), is to show the following is positive:

      \[\begin{align*} &\mu(0,0,0,1)-\frac{1+|\delta|}{2} \\ =&1-\alpha-\frac{1+|\delta|}{2} \\ >&1-\alpha-\frac{1+\max(\bar{\delta})}{2} \\ =&1-\alpha-\frac{1+\max\{\frac{2\alpha-1}{2},\frac{1-2\alpha}{2}\}}{2} \end{align*}\]

      From Part (i), we know that for \(\mu(0,0,0,1)>\frac{1+|\delta|}{2}>\frac{1}{2}\), it must be that \(\alpha<1/2\). Therefore \(\frac{2\alpha-1}{2}<\frac{1-2\alpha}{2}\). The expression above:

      \[\begin{align*} =&1-\alpha-\frac{1+\frac{1-2\alpha}{2}}{2} \\ =&1-\alpha-\frac{3-2\alpha}{4} \\ =&\frac{4-4\alpha-3+2\alpha}{4} \\ =&\frac{1-2\alpha}{4}. \end{align*}\]

      For this to be \(>0\):

      \[\begin{align*} \frac{1-2\alpha}{4}&>0 \\ 1-2\alpha&>0 \\ \alpha&<\frac{1}{2} \end{align*}\]

      Therefore the commoner only votes for \(P\) when \(\alpha<1/2\) in this case.

    In the first case, the reasoning is similar:

    • When \(s_{i,C}=1\), to prove \(\mu(1,1,1,0)<\frac{1-|\delta|}{2}\):

      \[\begin{align*} &\mu(1,1,1,0) \\ =&\frac{Pr(1,1,1,0\mid\omega_C=1)Pr(\omega_C=1)}{Pr(1,1,1,0\mid\omega_C=1)Pr(\omega_C=1)+Pr(1,1,1,0\mid\omega_C=0)Pr(\omega_C=0)} \\ =&\frac{Pr(1,\omega_E=1,s_{P,C}=0\mid1)}{Pr(1,\omega_E=1,s_{P,C}=0\mid1)+Pr(1,\omega_E=1,s_{P,C}=0\mid0)} \\ =&\frac{Pr(1\mid1)Pr(\omega_E=1\mid1)Pr(s_{P,C}=0\mid1)}{Pr(1\mid1)Pr(\omega_E=1\mid1)Pr(s_{P,C}=0\mid1)+Pr(1\mid0)Pr(\omega_E=1\mid0)Pr(s_{P,C}=0\mid0)}\\ =&\frac{p(1-p)\alpha}{p(1-p)\alpha+p(1-p)(1-\alpha)}\\ =&\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+(1-\alpha)}\\ =&\alpha \end{align*}\]

      Therefore, we need to show: \(\alpha-\frac{1-|\delta|}{2}<0\).

      • When \(\alpha>0.5\), \(|\delta|<\frac{2\alpha-1}{2}\), the expression above:

        \[\begin{align*} <&\alpha-\frac{1-\frac{2\alpha-1}{2}}{2} \\ =&\alpha-\frac{3-2\alpha}{4} \\ =&\alpha-\frac{3}{4}+\frac{1}{2}\alpha \\ =&\frac{3}{2}\alpha-\frac{3}{4} \end{align*}\]

        To show this is less than 0, we need to show:

        \[ \begin{align*} \frac{3}{2}\alpha-\frac{3}{4}&<0 \\ \frac{3}{2}\alpha&<\frac{3}{4} \\ \alpha<\frac{1}{2} \end{align*} \]
